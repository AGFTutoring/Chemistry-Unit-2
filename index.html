Energetics

Enthalpy Change

Enthalpy change, ΔH, is the heat energy transferred at constant pressure. A negative ΔH indicates an exothermic reaction (heat released to the surroundings); a positive ΔH indicates an endothermic reaction (heat absorbed from the surroundings).

Standard conditions for thermochemical measurements are 298 K, 100 kPa, with solutions at 1 mol/dm³.

ΔHf° — Formation
Enthalpy change when one mole of a compound forms from its elements in their standard states.
ΔHc° — Combustion
Enthalpy change when one mole of a substance burns completely in oxygen.
ΔHat° — Atomisation
Enthalpy change when one mole of gaseous atoms forms from an element in its standard state.
ΔHneut° — Neutralisation
Enthalpy change when an acid and base react to form one mole of water.

Hess's Law

The total enthalpy change for a reaction is independent of the route taken, provided the initial and final conditions are the same. This allows enthalpy changes that can't be measured directly to be calculated via an indirect cycle.

ΔHreaction = Σ(bonds broken) − Σ(bonds formed)
Tip: bond enthalpies used in this calculation are mean values averaged across many compounds, so the result is only an estimate — it will rarely match the experimentally measured value exactly. Only diatomic molecules (e.g. H–H, Cl–Cl) have a single, exact bond enthalpy.

Calorimetry

q = mcΔT    then    ΔH = −q / n

where q = heat energy transferred (J), m = mass of solution (g), c = specific heat capacity, ΔT = temperature change, n = moles of the limiting reactant. The negative sign in the second equation reflects that heat released by the reaction (q positive, exothermic) corresponds to a negative ΔH for the system.

Common source of error: heat loss to the surroundings during the experiment usually means the measured |ΔH| is an underestimate of the true value — calorimetry experiments should be extrapolated back to the moment of mixing to minimise this.
02

Intermolecular Forces

London (induced dipole) forces
Present between all molecules. Arise from temporary fluctuations in electron distribution. Strength increases with the number of electrons (and hence Mr) and with surface area of contact.
Permanent dipole-dipole forces
Occur between polar molecules, where one end is permanently δ+ and the other δ−, adding an attractive force on top of London forces.

Hydrogen Bonding

A particularly strong form of permanent dipole-dipole attraction, occurring when a hydrogen atom is bonded directly to a highly electronegative atom (F, O, or N) which also has a lone pair available to attract that hydrogen on a neighbouring molecule.

Why water's boiling point is unusually high: water forms an extensive 3D network of hydrogen bonds (each molecule can both donate and accept two hydrogen bonds), requiring far more energy to break than the London/dipole forces alone would predict for a molecule of its size.

Effect on Physical Properties

Stronger intermolecular forces generally mean higher melting and boiling points, since more energy is needed to separate the particles. This explains trends such as why boiling point increases with chain length in a homologous series (more electrons, stronger London forces) but decreases with branching (smaller surface area of contact between molecules).

Redox Chemistry & Groups 1 & 2

Redox: Oxidation & Reduction

OIL RIG  —  Oxidation Is Loss (of electrons), Reduction Is Gain

Oxidation states are assigned by convention: elements in their natural state = 0; the oxidation state of a simple ion equals its charge; oxygen is usually −2 (except in peroxides, where it is −1); hydrogen is usually +1 (except in metal hydrides, where it is −1); fluorine is always −1.

Half-equations are balanced by first balancing atoms, then balancing charge by adding electrons. Disproportionation occurs when the same element is simultaneously oxidised and reduced within one reaction.

Group 2 Trends & Reactions

Going down Group 2: atomic radius increases, ionisation energy decreases, and reactivity increases (the outer electrons are progressively easier to remove as they are further from the nucleus and more shielded).

Group 7 (The Halogens)

Going down Group 7: atomic radius increases, electronegativity decreases, and reactivity (as oxidising agents) decreases — the opposite trend to Group 2, since halogens react by gaining rather than losing electrons.

Displacement Reactions

A more reactive (more oxidising) halogen will displace a less reactive halogen from solution as the halide ion:

Cl₂ + 2Br⁻ → 2Cl⁻ + Br₂   (solution turns orange)
Cl₂ + 2I⁻ → 2Cl⁻ + I₂   (solution turns brown)

This reflects that reducing power of the halide ions increases down the group: Cl⁻ < Br⁻ < I⁻.

The precipitates can be distinguished further by their differing solubility in ammonia solution.

Chlorine in Water

Cl₂ + H₂O → HOCl + HCl

This is a disproportionation reaction (chlorine is simultaneously oxidised and reduced) and is the basis for chlorine's use in water purification, since HOCl is an effective disinfectant.

Introduction to Kinetics & Equilibria

Collision Theory

For a reaction to occur, particles must collide with both sufficient energy (at least the activation energy, Ea) and the correct orientation. Increasing temperature, concentration, pressure (for gases), or surface area (for solids) all increase the frequency of successful collisions and therefore the rate of reaction.

The Maxwell-Boltzmann Distribution

This distribution shows the spread of kinetic energies among particles in a sample at a given temperature. Raising the temperature shifts the whole curve to the right and flattens it, meaning a greater proportion of particles now have energy ≥ Ea — this is why even a modest temperature increase can have a large effect on reaction rate.

Tip: a catalyst does not change the shape of the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution itself — instead, it provides an alternative reaction pathway with a lower activation energy, so a larger proportion of the existing distribution already qualifies as "successful" collisions.

Le Chatelier's Principle

If a system at dynamic equilibrium is subjected to a change in concentration, temperature, or pressure, the position of equilibrium shifts to counteract that change.

ChangeEquilibrium response
Increase concentration of a reactantShifts right (towards products) to use up the excess
Increase temperatureShifts in the endothermic direction to absorb the extra heat
Increase pressure (gases)Shifts towards the side with fewer gas moles
Common exam trap: a catalyst speeds up the rate at which equilibrium is reached but does NOT shift the position of equilibrium itself — it speeds up the forward and reverse reactions equally.

Organic Chemistry: Halogenoalkanes, Alcohols & Spectra

General Reaction Types

Before getting into the specific chemistry, it helps to recognise the broad categories a reaction can fall into: addition (reactants combine into a single product), elimination (a small group is removed, often forming a double bond), substitution (one functional group replaces another), and hydrolysis (a molecule is broken apart by reaction with water).

Bond breaking is described as either homolytic (each atom keeps one electron from the shared pair, forming two radicals) or heterolytic (one atom takes both electrons, forming a positive and a negative ion). Heterolytic fission is what generates the electrophiles and nucleophiles central to this topic — an electrophile accepts an electron pair to form a new bond, while a nucleophile donates one.

Halogenoalkanes

A halogenoalkane has a polar carbon-halogen bond, since halogens are more electronegative than carbon — this leaves the carbon atom δ+ and open to nucleophilic attack. Halogenoalkanes are classified as primary, secondary, or tertiary depending on how many carbon groups are attached to the carbon bearing the halogen.

Reactivity trend: going down Group 7, the carbon-halogen bond gets weaker (despite becoming less polar) because the larger halogen atom forms a longer, weaker bond. A weaker bond breaks more easily, so iodoalkanes react fastest and fluoroalkanes slowest — bond strength, not polarity, controls the rate here.

Nucleophilic substitution reactions of halogenoalkanes

ReagentProduct

By contrast, heating a halogenoalkane with ethanolic KOH favours elimination rather than substitution — here the hydroxide ion acts as a base, removing a hydrogen and forming an alkene instead.

Testing halogenoalkanes with silver nitrate

Warming a halogenoalkane with aqueous silver nitrate (in ethanol, to keep everything in one phase) causes hydrolysis: water acts as the nucleophile, releasing the halide ion, which then precipitates with the silver ion. The precipitate colour identifies the halogen (white = chloride, cream = bromide, yellow = iodide), and the speed at which the precipitate appears reflects bond strength — iodide precipitates fastest, chloride slowest, consistent with the reactivity trend above.

Alcohols

Alcohols carry the −OH functional group and, like halogenoalkanes, are classified as primary, secondary, or tertiary depending on the substitution at the carbon bearing the hydroxyl group.

Making halogenoalkanes from alcohols

The −OH group of an alcohol can itself be substituted for a halogen:

Elimination: dehydration to alkenes

Heating an alcohol with concentrated phosphoric acid removes a molecule of water, forming an alkene — the reverse, in effect, of the acid-catalysed hydration of alkenes to alcohols seen in WCH11.

Oxidation of alcohols

Acidified potassium dichromate(VI) is the standard oxidising agent for this topic — it changes colour from orange to green as it is reduced, which itself is a visible indicator that oxidation has occurred.

Key Experimental Techniques

Reflux
Continuous heating with a condenser returning vapour to the flask, allowing a reaction to run to completion (e.g. full oxidation to a carboxylic acid) without losing volatile product.
Distillation
Separates liquids by boiling point — the lower-boiling component vaporises first and is condensed separately, isolating it from the mixture (e.g. collecting an aldehyde before it over-oxidises).
Separating funnel
Separates two immiscible liquids of different density into distinct layers, which can then be run off individually via the tap.
Drying
An anhydrous salt (commonly anhydrous sodium sulfate) is added to absorb residual water from an organic product, improving purity.

Boiling point is also used as a purity check: the closer the measured boiling point is to the literature value, and the narrower the range over which it occurs, the purer the sample.

Mass Spectrometry & Infrared Spectroscopy

Mass spectrometry (covered in more depth for atomic structure in WCH11) can also identify organic molecules from their molecular ion peak. One subtlety worth knowing: occasionally a doubly-charged ion (2+) forms during ionisation, which appears on the spectrum at exactly half its true mass-to-charge ratio — recognising this prevents misreading the molecular mass.

Infrared spectroscopy identifies functional groups by the characteristic wavenumber at which different bonds absorb IR radiation:

Bond / groupAbsorption range (cm⁻¹)
O–H (alcohol)3230 – 3550, broad
O–H (carboxylic acid)2500 – 3300, very broad
C=C1620 – 1680
C=O1680 – 1750

Below roughly 1500 cm⁻¹ lies the fingerprint region — a complex pattern unique to each specific molecule, useful for confirming an exact identity (e.g. by comparison against a reference spectrum) rather than for identifying individual functional groups.

Common exam trap: the alcohol O–H and acid O–H absorptions both sit in a similar broad region — distinguishing them relies on the acid peak extending further down (to ~2500 cm⁻¹) and the presence or absence of an accompanying C=O peak around 1700 cm⁻¹, which confirms a carboxylic acid rather than a simple alcohol.
